01 foundation/market-definition/SKILL.md

Market Definition

The foundational reference for every other skill in this system. Defines what business we are in, who we truly serve, what our students are actually buying, and who we genuinely compete with.

Purpose

This skill is the foundational reference for every other skill in this system. No positioning, messaging, campaign, or content decision should be made without first consulting this skill. It defines the market we compete in at the level of outcome — not product, not category label — and must be treated as the anchor of all strategic thinking.

What Business Are We In
To be defined through research
The single sentence that describes what problem we solve and for whom. Not our product. Not our category label. The outcome we deliver in the student's life. This must be expressed in student language derived from research — not institutional language derived from internal consensus.
Why this matters: How we answer this question determines who our real competitors are, what problem our marketing must solve, what category frame we compete in, and therefore every positioning and messaging decision that follows. Getting this wrong means the entire strategy is aimed at the wrong target.
Who We Serve

Our student population is not homogeneous. It contains two fundamentally different audiences making different decisions in different contexts. They must never be treated as one market.

Primary — Adult Learners Online
Approximately 80% of total students. Majority are over 30. Studying online while managing existing careers, family commitments, and financial obligations. Making a deliberate, high-stakes life decision. Long consideration period. High motivation once in market.
Secondary — School Leavers Campus
Approximately 20% of total students. On campus or hybrid. First experience of higher education in most cases. Different motivations, different influences, different fears, different decision process. Must not receive messaging designed for adult learners.
What Our Students Are Actually Buying

Students do not buy degrees. They buy outcomes they believe a degree will deliver. For adult learners the motivation is a combination of the following — the weighting between them differs by individual and must be defined per segment through research.

  • →Credential and formal qualification that opens a door currently closed to them
  • →Career advancement — promotion, salary increase, expanded role
  • →Career protection — staying relevant, future-proofing against displacement
  • →Career change — moving to a new field entirely
  • →Competitive advantage over peers and colleagues
  • →Identity and confidence — becoming the kind of person who has this qualification
To be defined through research
The actual weighting of these motivations by audience segment. The primary motivation that drives the decision to act. The language students use to describe what they are trying to achieve.
Our True Competitive Set
To be defined through research
Who students seriously considered before choosing us. Who they chose us over and why. Who chose a competitor instead of us and why. What switching to a non-university alternative looks like. Defined per audience segment.
The invisible competitor: For many adult learners the real competitor is not another institution — it is inertia. The decision to delay, defer, or abandon the idea of studying entirely. This competitor must be treated with the same strategic seriousness as any named institution.
How Students Define The Category
To be defined through research
The exact words and language students use when describing their decision to study. Do they say degree, course, uni, study, upskilling, training? This language must be used in all communications. Our language must match their language precisely.
The Nature of The Decision
To be defined through research
How long the consideration period typically is before action. Who else is involved in the decision. What the triggering moment looks like. What finally converts consideration into an application.
Strategic Constraints

This skill must be reviewed and potentially updated when any of the following occur:

  • !New audience research is completed that changes our understanding of motivation or decision-making
  • !A new audience segment is added to marketing scope
  • !A significant market shift occurs — economic, regulatory, competitive, or technological
  • !Enrolment trends diverge meaningfully from expectations in ways that suggest our market understanding is wrong
AI Collaborator Instructions
Before producing any output, check this skill against the following:
  • →Does this output speak to the right audience segment with language appropriate to that segment?
  • →Does it address what students are actually buying — outcomes — rather than what we are selling?
  • →Is it using the language students use to describe their decision, not institutional language?
  • →Does it acknowledge the right competitive context including the invisible competitor of inertia?
  • →Is it calibrated to where this student is in their decision journey?

02 foundation/objectives-and-measurement/SKILL.md

Objectives & Measurement

Defines what success looks like for this marketing function. The calibration point for every activity, campaign, and report. All execution skills trace their objectives back to this skill.

Purpose

This skill defines what success looks like for this marketing function. It is the calibration point for every activity, every campaign, and every report in this system. Measurement is not a reporting function — it is a strategic function embedded in every element of this system.

The Ultimate Objective

Generate new commencing students and the revenue they represent for the university, within each intake period. Every marketing activity must be traceable to this objective — directly or indirectly through brand health.

Marketing's Scope

Marketing owns the complete journey from first awareness to first unit enrolment. Responsibility transfers to the retention team at first unit enrolment. This boundary is fixed and must not creep in either direction.

The Two Objective Types
Brand Health Objectives
Move slowly. Investment may take 12–24 months to show measurable shift. They are the conditions that make commercial objectives achievable over time. Cannot be sacrificed for short-term commercial pressure without strategic sign-off. Measured quarterly, trended annually.
Commercial Objectives
Move faster. Measurable week to week and intake to intake. Directly tied to new commencing student numbers and revenue. Must be reported per intake period with trend visibility across intakes. Leading indicators monitored continuously.
The Re-Entry Loops

The pipeline is not strictly linear. Three specific re-entry points exist where prospects who have stalled are re-engaged by marketing. These represent the highest-value conversion activity in the entire pipeline — the prospect has already raised their hand.

Incomplete Application
Started but not submitted. The student demonstrated intent. Re-engagement must be timely, relevant, and frictionless. Must not feel punitive or pressuring.
Offer Follow-up
Received an offer but has not responded. This student has cleared academic requirements. Re-engagement must address the real barrier — which is rarely academic and usually emotional, financial, or logistical.
Enrolment Conversion
Accepted but not completed unit enrolment. Almost at the line. Re-engagement must be practical, clear, and supportive. Remove every possible friction from completion.
The Measurement Principle

Measurement is not a reporting function. It is a strategic function embedded in every element of this system. Every execution skill carries its own measurement. All measurement rolls up to the objectives defined in this skill. An activity that cannot be connected to an objective in this skill should not exist.

Indicator typeDefinitionExampleCadence
LeadingSignals something is working before outcomes are confirmedWebsite traffic, enquiry volume, application starts, brand search volumeWeekly

The Tension To Manage

Brand investment and commercial conversion operate on fundamentally different time horizons and require different types of resource and activity. This tension must be explicitly managed — it will not resolve itself.

The rule: If leadership only watches enrolment numbers, the brand investment that makes enrolment easier gets cut. If marketing only watches brand metrics, accountability for commercial outcomes is lost. Both must be held simultaneously.

03 foundation/competitive-landscape/SKILL.md

Competitive Landscape

Defines who we compete with, how we compete, and the conditions under which we win or lose. A living document — the competitive environment shifts and this skill must shift with it.

Purpose

This skill defines who we compete with, how we compete, and the conditions under which we win or lose. It is a living document — the competitive environment shifts and this skill must be updated when it does. No campaign, message, or positioning decision should be made without understanding the competitive context defined here.

Direct Competitors
To be defined through research
Other universities and higher education providers targeting the same audiences with comparable qualifications. For each direct competitor define: who they primarily serve, how they position themselves, where they are stronger than us, where we are stronger than them, and the conditions under which a student chooses them over us.
Indirect Competitors
To be defined through research
Providers who solve the same underlying problem through a different product. Includes vocational training providers, professional development programs, short course and micro-credential providers, bootcamps, and employer-funded training. These are increasingly important as adult learners have more alternatives to a full degree than at any previous point.
The Invisible Competitor

Inertia. Doing nothing. For many adult learners the real competitor is not another institution — it is the decision to delay, defer, or abandon the idea of studying entirely. This competitor must be treated as seriously as any named institution.

Why this is the most important competitor: You cannot win against inertia with features or price. You win by making the cost of not acting feel real and urgent and personal. This requires a different type of message than competitive comparison.
How We Compete
To be defined through research
The specific dimensions on which we are genuinely differentiated. Not claims we make — actual differences students experience before, during, and after studying with us. Must be expressed in student language not institutional language. Must be verifiable — every competitive claim must have proof.
Where We Win
To be defined through research and win/loss analysis
The specific conditions — audience type, motivation, situation, life stage, course category — under which we consistently win against competitors. These are the conditions we should seek to create more of through marketing activity.
Where We Lose
To be defined through research and win/loss analysis
The specific conditions under which we consistently lose. Understanding loss is as strategically important as understanding wins — arguably more so. Must be approached with honesty. Comfortable answers that protect internal sensitivities are not acceptable here.
The discipline required: Where we lose must be answered honestly or this skill has no strategic value. Rationalising losses as "not our target market" produces a strategy built on false foundations.
What We Will Not Do
To be defined as positioning decisions are made
Competitive positioning requires explicit choice. Being differentiated means deliberately deciding what we are not, what we will not claim, and which audiences we will not pursue — in order to own something specific. A strategy that tries to be everything to everyone owns nothing.
Competitive Signals To Monitor
  • →New entrants to the market — particularly non-university providers entering the adult learner space
  • →Competitor positioning and messaging changes — what are they now claiming?
  • →Competitor pricing and offer structure changes
  • →Competitor product and course catalogue changes
  • →Shifts in student perception of competitors — from brand tracking research
  • →Emerging alternative pathways that bypass university entirely — micro-credentials, employer credentials, AI-enabled self-education
  • →Regulatory changes that alter the competitive landscape
AI Collaborator Instructions
Before producing any output, check this skill against the following:
  • →Does this output acknowledge the real competitive context for the audience it is addressing?
  • →Does it speak to why us over the specific alternative this audience would consider?
  • →Does it address the invisible competitor — inertia — where relevant to the funnel stage?
  • →Does it avoid competing on dimensions where we do not genuinely win?
  • →Is every competitive claim supported by verifiable proof?
Flag and do not proceed if competitive claims cannot be substantiated or if the output inadvertently reinforces a competitor's position.
04 foundation/value-proposition/SKILL.md

Value Proposition

The bridge between what we know about our market and what we say to it. Defines the specific value we deliver to students, expressed in their language not ours. The honest answer to why us.

Purpose

This skill defines the specific value we deliver to our students, expressed in their language not ours. The value proposition is not a tagline. It is not a mission statement. It is the honest answer to the question every prospective student is asking: why should I choose this, and why should I believe you?

The Core Value Proposition
To be defined through research
A clear, specific, believable statement of the value we deliver. Derived from research. Expressed in student language. Must answer three things simultaneously: what we deliver, who it is for specifically, and why we are the best choice to deliver it. It must be specific enough to be meaningful, differentiated enough to be ownable, and credible enough to be believable.
Value By Audience Segment

The same institution delivers different value to different audiences. The core value proposition has one answer — but its expression must be calibrated to each segment.

Adult Learners — Over 30, Online
To be defined
This segment carries a full life alongside study. Value must speak to: what studying makes possible, why our model fits their life, what the return on their investment looks like, and why now is the right time.
Adult Learners — Under 30, Online
To be defined
Earlier in career. Different risk profile, different motivations, different competitive context. Value proposition must be calibrated accordingly.
School Leavers — On Campus
To be defined
Fundamentally different decision context. Likely first experience of higher education. Different influences, different fears, different definition of value. Must not be served the adult learner proposition.
The Jobs To Be Done

Students hire us to do specific jobs in their lives. Our value proposition must speak to all three layers of the job — weighted according to what research tells us matters most to each segment.

Functional Jobs
The practical outcomes they need. A qualification. A credential that opens a specific door. Skills that make them more capable at work. A new career pathway.
Emotional Jobs
How they want to feel as a result. Confident. Credible. Future-proof. Like they took action when it mattered. No longer behind. Proud.
Social Jobs
How they want to be perceived by others. By their employer, their peers, their family. The kind of person who has this qualification. Someone who invested in themselves.
To be defined through research
The specific weighting of functional, emotional, and social jobs by audience segment. The primary job driver for the decision to enrol. The language students use to describe each layer.
The Proof Layer

A value proposition without proof is a claim. Every element of our value proposition must be supported by evidence.

To be defined
Specific proof points per proposition element. Graduate outcome data. Employer feedback. Student testimonials that are specific not generic. Third-party validation where available. Without this layer the value proposition cannot be used in communications without making unsubstantiated claims.
Testing The Value Proposition
  • ?Is it specific enough to be meaningful — or could any university say exactly this?
  • ?Is it believable given who we are and what we can substantiate?
  • ?Is it differentiated from what our direct and indirect competitors claim?
  • ?Does it speak to what students actually care about — not what we want them to care about?
  • ?Would a prospective student recognise themselves and their situation in it?
  • ?Does it hold up when a student investigates further?
  • ?Does it address functional, emotional, and social jobs?
AI Collaborator Instructions
Before producing any output, check this skill against the following:
  • →Does this output deliver the value proposition or inadvertently undermine it?
  • →Is it speaking to the right segment with the right expression of the proposition?
  • →Is it using student language — not institutional language?
  • →Is every claim supported by the proof layer defined in this skill?
  • →Does it address functional, emotional, and social jobs — or only functional?
Flag and do not proceed if any output makes a claim that cannot be supported by the proof layer, or relies on generic claims defined as table stakes.
05 foundation/positioning/SKILL.md

Positioning

How we want to be known in the mind of our prospective student, relative to every alternative they could choose. A strategic choice made before any creative or messaging work begins.

Purpose

This skill defines how we want to be known in the mind of our prospective student, relative to every alternative they could choose. Positioning is a strategic choice — not a creative choice. It is decided before any creative or messaging work begins. Everything creative, content-related, and campaign-related in this system must express this positioning and never contradict it.

What Positioning Is Not
  • ✗Positioning is not a tagline or a headline
  • ✗Positioning is not a brand identity or a visual direction
  • ✗Positioning is not a list of features, benefits, or proof points
  • ✗Positioning is not something that changes with each campaign or each year
  • ✗Positioning is not what we want to be — it is the strategic choice of what we will consistently be in order to own a specific place in a specific mind relative to specific alternatives
Our Positioning Statement

The positioning statement is an internal strategic tool — not used verbatim in external communications. Everything external must be consistent with it.

For [specific audience]
who [specific situation or need]
we are the [category or frame of reference]
that [primary benefit or differentiation]
unlike [named or implied alternative]
we [specific proof point or reason to believe]
To be defined — after market definition, competitive landscape, and value proposition are complete
The positioning statement cannot be written until the preceding foundation skills have been completed and validated through research. Attempting to write it before then produces positioning built on assumption rather than insight.
The Position We Are Claiming
To be defined
The single idea we want to own in the market. It must be specific enough to be meaningful. Differentiated enough to be ownable. Credible enough to be believable. Valuable enough to drive preference over alternatives. One position — not a list of positions.
Positioning Through The Funnel

Our positioning must work at every stage of the funnel — but its expression adapts to where the student is in their decision journey. The underlying position never changes. The way it is expressed does.

Awareness
The position creates recognition and curiosity. Plant the idea. Make us memorable for a specific reason.
Consideration
The position creates relevance and resonance. Make the student feel seen. Connect our position to their specific situation.
Investigation
The position is validated by evidence and proof. Everything they find when they look closer must reinforce — never contradict — the position.
Preference
The position creates conviction. Make the choice feel obvious. Give them the language to explain their choice to others.
Conversion
The position removes doubt and justifies the decision. Reinforce that this was the right choice. Eliminate the last barriers.
Positioning Integrity
  • ✗Inconsistent messaging across channels that creates a fragmented picture of who we are
  • ✗Creative that prioritises novelty or awards over strategic consistency
  • ✗Claims that contradict or dilute the position we have chosen to own
  • ✗Campaigns that chase short-term conversion at the expense of long-term position without strategic sign-off
  • ✗Internal stakeholder pressure to represent every faculty or school equally regardless of strategic priority — one of the most common and most damaging sources of positioning erosion in university marketing
  • ✗Repositioning without strategic process — changing position because someone is bored with it or a new leader wants to make their mark
AI Collaborator Instructions
Before producing any output, check this skill against the following:
  • →Does this output express our position or contradict it?
  • →Does it speak to the right audience within our positioning frame?
  • →Does it express the position appropriately for the funnel stage it is targeting?
  • →Is it consistent with every other output produced by this system?
  • →Is this output responding to internal stakeholder pressure in a way that compromises positioning integrity?
Flag and do not proceed if any output contradicts the positioning statement, or appears to be driven by internal stakeholder pressure rather than strategic direction.
06 foundation/brand-strategy/SKILL.md

Brand Strategy

The long-term relationship between this institution and its audiences. Not visual identity. The emotional contract, the personality, the voice, and the promise that is kept consistently across every touchpoint over time.

Purpose

This skill defines the long-term relationship between this institution and its audiences. Brand strategy is not visual identity — that is an expression of brand, not the strategy itself. Brand strategy is the emotional contract we make with our audience: what we stand for, how we behave, what we promise, and how we consistently show up across every touchpoint over time.

What Brand Strategy Is
Not visual identity
Logos, colours, and typography are expressions of brand. Brand strategy is what those expressions are communicating. One is a tool, the other is the decision about what the tool must say.
Not a campaign
Campaigns come and go. Brand is what persists across campaigns. Every campaign should build the brand — never contradict it.
Not a tagline
A tagline is an expression of a brand idea. The brand idea must exist and be strategically sound before a tagline is written.
What it is
The cumulative impression left in the mind of every person who encounters this institution across every touchpoint — and the strategic decisions about what that impression should be and how it is consistently created.
Brand Purpose
To be defined
Why this institution exists beyond commercial objectives. The contribution it makes to the lives of its students and to society. Not a marketing statement — a genuine answer to why this institution matters. Purpose must be believable given our history and our actual behaviour. Aspirational purposes that contradict lived experience destroy brand trust faster than having no stated purpose at all.
Brand Personality
To be defined
If this institution were a person, how would they behave? Personality defines the consistent human qualities that should be recognisable across every interaction. Defined as a small set of qualities with specific behavioural implications — not a generic list of adjectives.
The test of brand personality: Every quality must have a "not this, but this" definition. Not "professional" — that says nothing. "Straight-talking rather than corporate-formal" — that gives the team something to act on.
Brand Voice and Tone

Voice is consistent — it is who we are. Tone adapts to context — it is how we adjust our voice to the situation.

Voice
To be defined
The consistent qualities of how we write and speak. Must be specific enough that two different people writing independently produce outputs that feel like the same institution.
Tone — Awareness
To be defined
How the voice adapts when communicating with someone who barely knows us.
Tone — Conversion
To be defined
How the voice adapts when communicating with someone close to deciding. More specific, more proof-oriented, more direct about what happens next.
Tone — Re-entry Loops
To be defined
How the voice adapts when re-engaging someone who has stalled. Must be warm and frictionless — never pressuring, never guilt-inducing, never making them feel like a number in a pipeline.
Brand Promise
To be defined
The specific commitment we make to every student who chooses us. Not a marketing claim — an operational commitment that must be kept. The brand promise connects marketing to the institution — it is the contract between what we say and what we do.
The critical test: Can the institution actually deliver this promise to every student every time? If not, either the promise must change or the institution must change. Making a promise that is not kept is worse than making no promise.
Brand Pillars

The small number of core themes that anchor everything we communicate. Not a list of features — a set of strategic territories that every piece of content and communication should be able to connect to. Typically three to five.

01
[To be defined]
First brand pillar — a strategic territory that anchors a category of communication. Defined after positioning is complete.
02
[To be defined]
Second brand pillar. Each pillar should be distinct — no overlap. Overlap creates confusion not reinforcement.
03
[To be defined]
Third brand pillar. The complete set should feel like a coherent whole — aspects of one idea rather than a list of separate ideas.
04
[To be defined]
Fourth pillar if required. Should only exist if it represents a genuinely distinct strategic territory not covered by the preceding three.
What Damages Brand
  • ✗Inconsistency across touchpoints — the brand feels different depending on where you encounter it
  • ✗Overpromising in marketing that is not matched by the actual student experience
  • ✗Generic communications that could have come from any institution
  • ✗Reactive communications that chase trends at the expense of brand consistency
  • ✗Internal fragmentation — different faculties or departments projecting contradictory identities
  • ✗Crisis communications that abandon brand personality and revert to corporate defensive language
  • ✗Treating brand as a marketing function responsibility rather than an institutional responsibility
AI Collaborator Instructions
Before producing any output, check this skill against the following:
  • →Does this output reflect our brand personality — would a person with our defined qualities produce this?
  • →Is it written in our brand voice with the appropriate tone for this context and funnel stage?
  • →Does it connect to at least one brand pillar?
  • →Does it honour the brand promise — does it make or imply a commitment the institution can actually keep?
  • →Is it consistent with every other output in this system — does it feel like the same institution?
  • →Could this output have come from any university — or is it distinctly and recognisably ours?
Flag and do not proceed if any output feels generic, contradicts the brand personality, makes a promise the institution cannot keep, or could plausibly have come from a competitor. The test: remove the logo. Does it still feel like us?
